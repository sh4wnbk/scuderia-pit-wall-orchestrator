  9 | ## The Problem
 10 | 
 11 | ![The Synthesis Gap Visualization](assets/synthesis_gap.png)
 12 | 
 13 | Formula 1's 2026 regulations introduce the most technically complex power unit and aerodynamic rules in the sport's history. F1 cars generate **1.1 million telemetry data points per second**. Pit-wall radio is high-noise, jargon-heavy, and opaque to anyone without an engineering background.
 14 |

 16 | 
 17 | The current digital ecosystem offers three fragmented channels: a TV broadcast with no technical context, a live timing app with raw data and no explanation, and social media with delayed, unverified commentary. Fans are forced to act as their own data engineers, abandoning the live broadcast to hunt for answers — and missing the race while doing it.
 18 | 
 19 | This is the **Synthesis Gap**.
 20 |

 22 | 
 23 | ## The Solution
 24 | 
 25 | The Scuderia Pit-Wall Fan Orchestrator is a multi-agent AI companion powered by **IBM watsonx** that:
 26 | 
 27 | - Ingests 1.1M live telemetry data points per second via **FastF1**
 28 | - Transcribes and translates unstructured pit-wall radio in real time via **IBM Watson Speech-to-Text**
 29 | - Cross-references both against the **FIA 2026 Technical Regulations** using RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)
 30 | - Delivers plain-English tactical narratives via text and **IBM Watson Text-to-Speech** audio overlay
 31 | - Allows fans to query the system in natural language without looking away from the TV broadcast
 32 | - Enforces proactive governance via **IBM watsonx.governance** to guarantee no confident-wrong output ever reaches the fan
 33 | 
 34 | ![The Trust Output Matrix — why confident-wrong is an existential threat](assets/trust_output_matrix.png)
 35 | 
 36 | > *"Leclerc is harvesting battery on this straight. Ferrari is building toward a push lap. Expect a pit call within two laps."*

 40 | ## Architecture
 41 | 
 42 | ![Agentic Pipeline Architecture — corrected with governance layer](assets/architecture_diagram.png)
 43 | 
 44 | The system is built across six layers, each mapping to a named component:
 45 | 
 46 | ```
 47 | ① Perception          NLP Synthesizer + Watson Speech-to-Text
 48 | ② Memory & Knowledge  RAG Vector DB (FIA 2026 regs + strategy history)
 49 | ③ Reasoning           Planning Engine → Regulation Agent | Agentic Strategist
 50 | ④ Tools               FastF1 API (live telemetry)
 51 | ⑤ Governance          Proactive Overseer Agent (watsonx.governance)
 52 | ⑥ Action & Execution  Execution Module → Tactical Narrative + Audio Overlay
 53 | ```
 54 | 
 55 | ### The Governance Layer — Why It Exists
 56 | 
 57 | ![The Proactive Governance Loop — three trustworthy outcomes guaranteed](assets/governance_loop.png)
 58 | 
 59 | 
 60 | A confident-wrong answer is categorically more damaging than acknowledged uncertainty.
 61 | 
 62 | A fan who receives *"I cannot confirm this right now"* stays engaged. A fan who receives a confident wrong narrative, feels an emotion about it during a live race, shares it, and later discovers the truth on social media has been actively misled. Trust is gone permanently.
 63 | 
 64 | The **Proactive Overseer Agent** intercepts every output at three monitoring hooks before delivery:
 65 | 
 66 | - **Hook 1** — Transcript audit (NLP Synthesizer output)
 67 | - **Hook 2** — Retrieval audit (RAG Vector DB output)
 68 | - **Hook 3** — Pre-delivery cross-validation (citation verified against source)
 69 | 
 70 | If cross-validation fails, the Overseer triggers a retry with an amended context and specific failure reason — not a blind retry. If the retry budget is exhausted, the system delivers an honest uncertainty response rather than a confident wrong answer.
 71 | 
 72 | **Three possible outputs. No others.**
 73 | 
 74 | | Output | Condition | Trust outcome |
 75 | |---|---|---|
 76 | | Tactical narrative + audio overlay | Citation validated, confidence ≥ 0.7 | Trust built |
 77 | | Narrative + audit flag | Retry passed | Trust maintained |
 78 | | Uncertainty response | Retry budget exhausted | Trust preserved |
 79 | 
 80 | ### Latency Budget
 81 | 
 82 | | Component | Time |
 83 | |---|---|
 84 | | First pass (inference + RAG) | ~1,000ms |
 85 | | Overseer check | ~150ms |
 86 | | Retry with amended context | ~950ms |
 87 | | Re-check | ~150ms |
 88 | | **Total worst case** | **~2,250ms** |
 89 | 
 90 | The 250ms overrun against the 2-second target is an explicit design decision. A 2.25-second validated response is preferable to a 1.8-second confident-wrong one.

 39 | ## Overall Verdict
 40 | 
 41 | **Structurally sound, but with 4 notable misalignments.**
 42 | 
 43 | The diagram correctly identifies all five required layers. The dual-agent routing (Regulation Agent + Agentic Strategist) maps accurately to Week 2's Cluster 1 and Cluster 2 prioritisation. The red output nodes correctly represent the dual-state UX from Week 1 and Week 2. The foundation is right. The problems are in the connections and omissions.

 46 | 
 47 | ## Misalignment 1 — Speech-to-Text is in the wrong subgraph
 48 | 
 49 | **What the diagram shows:**  
 50 | Speech-to-Text lives inside the "Tools & External Interfaces" subgraph, connected to the NLP Synthesizer via the Tool Connectors node.
 51 | 
 52 | **Why this is wrong:**  
 53 | In the project's architecture, audio ingestion is a Perception layer concern. Speech-to-Text is not a tool that gets invoked on demand — it is an always-on component that feeds the input pipeline continuously during a race. Placing it in Tools implies it is optional, external, and called when needed. It is none of those things.
 54 | 
 55 | **The correct placement:**  
 56 | Speech-to-Text belongs inside the Perception subgraph, feeding directly into the NLP Synthesizer. This accurately reflects the continuous audio transcription pipeline that runs throughout the race session.
 57 | 
 58 | **What this misalignment understates:**  
 59 | If Speech-to-Text is in Tools, it appears to be the same category of thing as FastF1 API — an external data source queried on demand. But Speech-to-Text is actually half of the input layer alongside structured telemetry. The difference matters for latency architecture: an always-on stream has fundamentally different requirements from a request-response API call.
 60 | 
 61 | ---
 62 | 
 63 | ## Misalignment 2 — The user (Sofia) is absent from the diagram
 64 | 
 65 | **What the diagram shows:**  
 66 | The diagram has no user node. Information appears to begin at the Input Processor with no origin.
 67 | 
 68 | **Why this is wrong:**  
 69 | Sofia is not passive. Her natural language queries — the "pull" mechanism — are central to the product's value proposition. The Agentic Strategy Chat (Cluster 2, Rank 2 priority in Week 2) is specifically the mechanism by which fans pull context via natural language queries. If Sofia is not in the diagram, this entire interaction mode is invisible.
 70 | 
 71 | More critically, the invisible constraint from Sofia's persona card — *"if the AI hallucinates a strategy call or lags behind the live TV broadcast, trust is destroyed and the tool is permanently abandoned"* — has no meaning if the user is absent. The diagram should make visible who the system is serving and how they enter it.
 72 | 
 73 | **The correct addition:**  
 74 | Sofia appears as a distinct node (stadium shape, IBM Blue `#0f62fe`) with two incoming arrows: one for her voice/text query, and one representing the 1.1M pts/sec telemetry stream as a separate explicit entry point.
 75 | 
 76 | **Inference captured here:**  
 77 | The 1.1M telemetry figure is one of the most striking numbers in the entire project. It appears in the Week 1 and Week 2 presentations as a key data point justifying the AI approach. Leaving it out of the architecture diagram loses the visual argument for why a simple app cannot handle this — only an agentic AI system can.

 81 | ## Misalignment 3 — RAG connections are undirected
 82 | 
 83 | **What the diagram shows:**  
 84 | The RAG Database connects to the Regulation Agent and Agentic Strategist using `---` (undirected lines with labels "2026 FIA Regs" and "Strategy History").
 85 | 
 86 | **Why this is wrong:**  
 87 | Undirected connections suggest a bidirectional or ambiguous relationship. The actual relationship is directional: the agents query the RAG store, and the RAG store returns retrieved context. This is not a passive association — it is an active retrieval operation that is foundational to the system's accuracy and trustworthiness.
 88 | 
 89 | **The correct representation:**  
 90 | Directional arrows from the RAG DB to each agent, labeled with what is retrieved (`FIA 2026 regs`, `strategy history`). This makes the retrieval flow explicit and sets up the governance layer's audit function correctly — the Overseer needs to monitor what is retrieved, which implies retrieval is a discrete, observable event.
 91 | 
 92 | **Inference captured here:**  
 93 | The direction matters architecturally. RAG retrieval happens before reasoning, not after. An agent that reasons first and retrieves second is confirming its own assumptions. An agent that retrieves first is constrained by what the documents actually say. Making the arrows directional makes this sequencing visible.
 94 |

 97 | ## Misalignment 4 — The 1.1M telemetry source has no representation
 98 | 
 99 | **What the diagram shows:**  
100 | The Input Processor exists but has no labeled input. The telemetry stream — the primary continuous data source — is implied but invisible.
101 | 
102 | **Why this is wrong:**  
103 | The entire justification for an agentic AI system (rather than a smarter app) is the scale and velocity of the data. 1.1 million data points per second is not a number that can be handled by a traditional request-response architecture. Making this visible in the architecture diagram is the primary argument for the technical approach chosen. Leaving it out loses that argument.

105 | **The correct addition:**  
106 | An explicit `Telem[1.1M pts/sec]` node with a labeled arrow (`raw telemetry`) into the Input Processor. This appears alongside Sofia's query entry point — two distinct input streams, both explicit.

172 | ## Architecture — Six Layers
173 | 
174 | ```
175 | ① Perception       NLP Synthesizer + Watson STT (always-on audio)
176 | ② Memory           RAG Vector DB — retrieve FIRST, reason SECOND
177 | ③ Reasoning        Planning Engine → Regulation Agent | Agentic Strategist

180 | ⑥ Action           Execution Module → Tactical Narrative + Audio Overlay
181 | ```
182 | 
183 | **Three output states (no others):**
184 | - `confirmed` — citation validated, Overseer approved
185 | - `retry_corrected` — failed first pass, retry passed
186 | - `uncertainty` — retry budget exhausted, honest fallback
187 | 
188 | **Governance — Three Hooks:**
189 | - Hook 1: NLP Synthesizer → Overseer (transcript audit)
190 | - Hook 2: RAG Store → Overseer (retrieval audit)
191 | - Hook 3: Execution Module → Overseer (pre-delivery cross-validation)
192 | 
193 | **Retry loop:** Overseer sends specific failure reason + amended context back to Planning. Max 2 retries. Not blind retry — informed retry.
194 | 
195 | **Latency budget:**
196 | - First pass: ~1,000ms
197 | - Overseer check: ~150ms
198 | - Retry: ~950ms
199 | - Re-check: ~150ms
200 | - Total worst case: ~2,250ms (intentionally over 2s target — trust > speed)

204 | ## Key Design Decisions
205 | 
206 | 1. **Retrieve before reason** — RAG query happens before Granite call. Enforced structurally in code, not just documented.
207 | 2. **Proactive not reactive governance** — Overseer intercepts before delivery. Reactive logs after the fact; too late for live sports.
